Notice for mouse coordinates


Selecting shows the position of the mouse in approximate Helioprojective Cartesian (HPC) coordinates. The position of the mouse is converted to HPC coordinates using the small-angle approximation as described in Section 4.1 of Thompson (2006).


Selecting shows the position of the mouse in approximate Helioprojective Radial (HPR) coordinates. The position of the mouse is converted to HPR coordinates using the small-angle approximation as described in Section 4.2 of Thompson (2006).


HPC and HPR coordinates are calculated under the assumption that the Sun is always 1 AU from the observer. Note that these coordinates are approximate only and should not be used for operational purposes, or to support more precise scientific data analysis.



Helioprojective Cartesian Coordinates

In helioprojective coordinates, everything in the sky is assumed to be projected onto a celestial sphere, as illustrated in the left-hand portion of the above graphic.

However, the Sun has a relatively small angular extent on the sphere when observed from the Earth. The full angular width of the Sun as seen from Earth is about half a degree. If we were to draw a line from Earth to the center of the Sun (with distance D), and from the Earth to a point on or close to the disk of Sun (at distance d from Earth), the angle between these lines would be small.

The right hand side of the above graphic shows that for small angles, the curve of the celestial sphere is approximately flat. In this situation, the angular separation between two points can be used as a measure of the physical distance between those points. The equation relating the distance X to the angle θx illustrates this, and can be derived trigonometrically.



Helioprojective Radial Coordinates

In this coordinate system, a radial representation is used to represent the same point rather than a cartesian representation. The circle is still mapped onto the same celestial sphere, which means that the distance from origin (ρ) is an approximation.



Please refer to Thompson (2006; DOI: 10.1051/0004-6361:20054262) for more information on solar coordinate systems.
